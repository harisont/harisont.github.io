With several papers in the making and my _kappa_[^1] to be tackled soon, this semester is my most writing-intensive so far.
In this blog post, I want to gather some thoughts about (academic) writing tips I received over the years and how they relate to my own writing process.

## Write now, edit later
<!--method-->
I read an email thread not too long ago where someone wrote that a draft is better than nothing.
In context, I could not disagree more, as the statement was about LLM-generated drafts.
However, it made me reflect to the "write now, edit later" strategy many people seem to swear by.
The idea is to write a first complete, rough draft of whatever you're writing as fast as possible and then, and only then, edit as much as needed, because a draft can be revised but a blank page can't.

<!--problem-->
While the latter is a true statement, this is definitely not how I work.
In fact, I do _not_ think a draft - whether or not it is automatically generated - is _necessarily_ better than nothing.
My problem with messy drafts is that they're difficult to read through.
"Write now, edit later" may help you overcome a writer's block, but it might also just postpone it to the revision stage.
Worse, your writer's block may be a consequence of not knowing exactly what to say and how to structure your text, in which case "write now, edit later" might lead to a draft that needs to be reworked completely, which ends up slowing the process down and cranking frustration up.

<!--what I do instead-->
Rather than writing drafts, I make increasingly detailed outlines: section headings, paragraph titles, bullet points, anything but a complete sentence.
These are typically nicely spaced (and therefore easy to read) and less painful to modify, move around, even delete, as they didn't take that much effort to write in the first place.
Once I'm reasonably satisfied with the outline, by definition I know what to write and where.
Writing becomes just a matter to figure out the how, and I take my time to do that so that I don't hate it when I inevitably need to edit it.
This method works very well for me, especially when working alone.

<!--however-->
However, I recently received a messy, yet very workable draft from one of my wonderful students and I finally understood the value of a rough draft.
It's not for the author - it's for the supervisor (or whoever is giving you early feedback)!
A draft can be revised and a blank page can't, sure, but in a sense, neither can a polished text.
I have occasionally been annoyed about some colleagues who only give me what seems to be superficial feedback about my paper drafts, but I'm starting to think it might depend on the fact that I never gave them actual drafts.
Feedback on a "finished" text is hard to deliver (I experienced that first-hand with an LLM-generated draft) and harder to receive and act upon.
Many times, when I receive good feedback, making changes feels like a daunting task even if I definitely want to incorporate it in my paper: every word has been chosen carefully, moving a sentence makes the structure of the whole paragraph collapse.
The text has already solidified into a certain shape and can't be molded into anything different anymore. 
The new shape needs to be carved out of what's already there.

<!--conclusion-->
In conclusion, my advice to myself and potentially others is: definitely write an outline, but try not to skip the draft stage if you actually want feedback.
